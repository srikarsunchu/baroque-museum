Masterworks in Focus

Theatrical Mythology: Jupiter Rebuked by Venus

Paolo de Matteis' grand mythological scene captures the height of Italian Baroque theatricality. Venus confronts Jupiter among swirling drapery and divine figures, demonstrating the period's love of dramatic narrative and emotional intensity. Created in the early 18th century, this work shows how Baroque principles persisted even as the era evolved, maintaining the grand manner painting tradition with spectacular color, dynamic composition, and mythological grandeur that educated viewers expected.

Chiaroscuro Mastery: Old Peasant Lighting a Pipe

Adriaen van Ostade's intimate genre scene demonstrates Dutch mastery of light and shadow. A single candle illuminates the peasant's weathered face, creating the dramatic contrasts that define Baroque painting. This modest subject—worlds apart from Italian mythological spectacle—reveals how Baroque techniques transformed everyday moments into profound artistic statements. Van Ostade elevates humble life through the same technical virtuosity reserved for kings and gods.

Classical Restraint: The Continence of Scipio

Nicolas Poussin brings French intellectual rigor to Baroque drama. This ancient Roman story of virtue and restraint unfolds with carefully orchestrated gestures and classical architecture. While embracing Baroque's emotional power and narrative clarity, Poussin tempers theatrical excess with rational composition. His work represents the "classical" strain of Baroque—where ancient ideals meet seventeenth-century technical mastery, creating paintings that engage both mind and heart.

Stories Across Europe

This collection reveals Baroque art's remarkable diversity. From Naples to Amsterdam, Paris to Haarlem, artists adapted shared principles—dramatic lighting, emotional intensity, technical mastery—to local traditions and patrons. Italian painters pursued mythological grandeur. Dutch masters elevated everyday moments. French academicians balanced passion with reason. Spanish artists captured dramatic narratives. Each geographic region contributed its own voice to the Baroque chorus.

These five works span nearly a century of artistic innovation: from Dirck Hals' elegant gatherings in 1630s Flanders, through Poussin's classical restraint in 1640 and Van Ostade's candlelit intimacy of the 1650s, to De Matteis' theatrical mythology in early 1700s Italy. Together, they trace how Baroque artistry evolved while maintaining its core commitment to engaging viewers emotionally, technically, and intellectually—making distant subjects feel immediate and transforming paint into experience.
