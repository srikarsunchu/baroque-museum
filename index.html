Five Masterpieces

These five paintings span nearly a century of European art, from the 1630s to the 1710s. They range from grand mythological scenes painted for Italian patrons to quiet Dutch interiors lit by a single candle. Each work demonstrates the Baroque period's characteristic use of dramatic lighting, emotional intensity, and technical skill.

Masterworks in Focus

Jupiter Rebuked by Venus

Paolo de Matteis painted this large mythological scene in Naples around 1706-1710. Venus stands at the center, gesturing toward Jupiter, who reclines among other gods and figures. The composition uses diagonal lines and strong contrasts of light and shadow. De Matteis trained in the tradition of Luca Giordano, and this work follows the grand manner style popular in Italian courts—large scale, classical subject matter, and rich color.

Old Peasant Lighting a Pipe

Adriaen van Ostade painted this small panel around 1660. A single candle lights the peasant's face, creating sharp contrasts between illuminated and shadowed areas. Van Ostade specialized in scenes of everyday life in Dutch villages. Here, he applies the same chiaroscuro techniques used in grand Italian paintings to a modest subject—a man lighting his pipe in a dim interior.

The Continence of Scipio

Nicolas Poussin painted this scene from Roman history in 1640. The composition arranges figures in a clear, balanced arrangement against classical architecture. Poussin spent most of his career in Rome, developing a style that combined Baroque drama with classical order. Unlike the more dynamic Italian Baroque, Poussin's work emphasizes rational composition and moral clarity.

Stories Across Europe

These five paintings show how Baroque art varied across Europe. Artists in different regions used similar techniques—dramatic lighting, strong emotion, technical skill—but applied them to different subjects and styles. Italian painters worked on large mythological scenes for court patrons. Dutch artists painted small scenes of everyday life for middle-class buyers. French painters favored classical subjects with balanced compositions.

The works span from the 1630s to the 1710s. Dirck Hals painted elegant company scenes in 1630s Flanders. Nicolas Poussin completed his Roman history painting in 1640. Adriaen van Ostade made his candlelit peasant scene in the 1650s. Paolo de Matteis finished his mythological scene in early 1700s Naples. Together, they show how Baroque painting changed over nearly a century while keeping its focus on dramatic lighting and emotional expression.
